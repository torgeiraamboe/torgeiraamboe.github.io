Here are some projects I could supervise and which I think could be interesting for bachelor students. 

## Brown’s representability theorem
    
Brown representability is one of the fundamental backbones of stable homotopy theory and is one of the prominent reasons why cohomology and homology are very interesting things to study when it comes to topological spaces. It is also generalizable to other settings, in particular triangulated categories, using the work by Neeman and Krause, and to infinity categories using the work of Lurie. The main part of the project will be to present a thorough proof of the theorem (there are some tricky and very non-trivial parts) and perhaps look at some interesting examples.
    
**Idea:** The functors from topological spaces to abelian groups that are cohomology theories, are representable functors. If one collects all the cohomology functors into a single functor into graded abelian groups, then the functor is represented by a spectrum object in topological spaces.
    
## The symmetric monoidal Barr-Beck theorem
    
This theorem is a very cool and useful theorem in homotopy theory which I have used several times in my research. It is originally stated using the language of $\infty$-categories, but the theorem should also be true in the setting of normal categories. The main part of the thesis will then be to prove this theorem in the setting of normal categories (one possible route to take is through Beck's monadicity theorem, a statement about which functors are monadic), and to look at some interesting examples where it applies. 
    
**Idea:** If a monoidal adjunction $F\colon C\leftrightarrows D\colon U$ is nice enough ($U$ preserves colimits, is conservative, and we have a projection formula; this is essentially saying it is monadic in a nice way), then the category $D$ is simply the category of modules over the ring object $U(1)$
    
## The Serre-Swan theorem
    
In my mind this theorem is one of the fundamental results that links algebra to algebraic geometry, topology, or differential geometry, depending on what you are most interested in. In algebraic topology the theorem states that a vector bundles over a nice topological space $T$ is equivalent to a finitely generated projectoive module over its ring of functions $C(T)$. In algebraic geometry one can state the result as there being an equivalence between finitely generated projective modules over a ring $R$ and algebraic vector bundles over $Spec(R)$. The main part of the project would then be to prove one of the two theorems, depending on the interest in either algebraic topology or algebraic geometry. 
    
**Idea:** Algebraic vector bundles (some sort of coherent sheaf) on an affine variety/scheme $X$ are equivalent to projective modules over the ring of global sections $\Gamma(X).$
    
## The $K(1)$-local sphere
    
This last suggested project is less about proving a theorem, and more about doing a certain calculation. The calculation is basically: figure out the stable homotopy groups of the spheres, localized at Morava K-theory at height $1$. This statement is quite difficult to grasp, and I think this project would feature several black-boxes about stable and chromatic homotopy theory. But, once all the theory is set up, the calculation is quite cool, and it reveals essentially all of the “lowest degree” periodicity in the homotopy groups of the spheres. It can also be done through the lens of more classical algebraic topology, i.e. by using the image of the $J$-homomorphism.
    
**Idea:** The $K(1)$-local sphere can be viewed as the fiber of a map between complex K-theory (normal, not Morava, i.e. isomorphism classes of vector bundles), and computing its homotopy groups is then reduced to computing the set of elements that gets sent to $1$ under the Adams operations on K-theory.
